Pro Set Platinum really pushed the envelope in 1991 with their premium Platinum set. By this point it was obvious that there was room in the hobby for "premium" sets, and Pro Set joined the fun with not one, but two premium offerings for the 1991-92 season (the other was the resurrection of the Parkhust brand). So, how far did Pro Set push the boundaries? Not only were the cards high-gloss on both sides, but the front of the cards featured full bleed action shots with no player name, or team logo, or position. Just an action shot and the silver Pro Set Platinum logo. Bold? Brave? Stupid? Maybe it's all three. This is probably one of the more divisive set designs from the era - you either love 'em or hate 'em. I suppose the idea at the time was to focus exclusively on the player - no other distractions (other than branding of course!) to take away from the raison d'Ãªtre of a hockey card - to show the player.

On to the set review...

The set itself was divided equally across two series of 150 cards each, which made collecting each series a fairly easy thing to do. Packs contained 12 cards each, 36 packs per box. Inserted into each series was a 10-card Platinum Collectibles set, for a total of 20 chase cards. The set was organised alphabetically by team, with about 6 or seven players featured per team, depending on the club. Subsets varied by Series, with a 6-card Platinum Performance set and a 6-card Original Six set in Series I, and Prospects, All Star, Performance, and Celebrity Captain cards in Series II. The Series I Performance set featured a card for each divisional champion, plus a card for Don Beaupre's league-leading shutout performance the previous season with five. The other six cards featured a static shot of each team's 75th NHL Anniversary throwback jersey (with hockey props!) and the Celebrity Captains subset in Series II. As weird as it looked in the set, apparently the NHL designated a celebrity "fan" of the club to act as an honorary captain for the season, again in celebration of the NHL's 75th. Believe it or the Fred Rogers card is one of the more valuable in the set. Go figure. Weirdly there are only 12 cards in the subset, yet we had 23 teams that year. Series II cards is the only place we find rookies in the set, as there were none in Series I. And the names are the cream of the crop from that year - Dominik Hasek, Nicklas Lidstrom, John LeClair, Tony Amonte, and Doug Weight are the stand-out rookies.

This set had real potential - but it has one fatal flaw (maybe two). If you are going to bet the farm on a set design which puts the player (and therfore the photography) front and centre, well, that photography better be world-class. You better have the very best shots & the very best digital processing money can buy, so that player leaps off the card and right into your kitchen. But that was not the case. In fact it was th exact opposite - cards were more often than not under-exposed, and the shot selection left a lot to be desired. Craig Janney's card for example is a shot of two Boston players side-by-side. Who should I be looking at? Which dude is Craig Janney? And if look closely at Petr Svoboda & Kirk Muller's cards you'll notice they were taken minutes apart from each other - both players are in front of the same advertising, same section of stands (those 100 level in the Reds at the old Forum were the bomb by the way) with the same guy in the background. Lazy? Maybe.

But it's not all bad though. Turgeon's card is awesome, as is the shot of Bill Ranford. Patrick Roy's landscape shot stopping Kip Miller in-close is great, so is Kay Whitmore's toe save, and Kirk McLean flashing the leather. But overall there are more forgettable cards than there are unbelievable cards. If the majority of the set looked like these, it would have been a real classic set.

I'm going to go out on a limb here and also suggest the backs of the cards were kinda ground-breaking too - the secondary player shot was another game action shot, and took up at least two thirds of the card. Only here will you find the player's name, position, team, card number, and small player fact. No stats. None. At all. Players were also given a few designations - Platinum Performer for regular player cards, Platinum Prospect for rookies & sophomores (plus the word "Prospect" above the roundel on the card front), Platinum All Star for, you guessed it, All Star Team nominations, and finally more Platinum Performance cards.

Best of The Set Awards:

Best Card: Shane Corson blowing a bubble...O-Pee-Chee gum perhaps?
Best Goalie Card: Glenn Healy...just another day at the rink
Worst Card: John Vanbiesbrouck...completely blocked by Patrik Sundstrom
Best Looking Rookie Card: Jim Paek! Hoisting the Cup! On yer Rookie card!

It's interesting to me that two card sets in 1991-92 came to market with full-bleed cards - Topps with Stadium Club, and Pro Set Platinum. While the two sets are similar in design, the two could not be further from each other in execution. The Platinum set was ground-braking in it's design, but fell flat with the execution. Player shots were under-exposed and confusing, Series I had exactly zero rookies, and they printed and printed and printed. I still have a Series II box unopened. For me this goes in the "curiosity" bucket - interesting, maybe has a bit of historical value, but that's about it. No wonder Pro Set only release it once.
